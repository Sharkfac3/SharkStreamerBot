# Brand Voice Guide

> **For agents:** This document defines how this brand sounds across different contexts. Load `BRAND-IDENTITY.md` first to understand why the brand sounds this way. This document tells you how to apply it in practice.

---

## The Voice in One Sentence

Chaotic, warm, self-aware, and genuinely knowledgeable — the voice of someone who knows their brain works differently, finds that funny, and is building something cool anyway.

---

## Core Voice Qualities

| Quality | What It Means | What to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Authentic** | Specific, honest, and personal — never generic | Corporate polish, vague aspirational language |
| **Self-aware** | Comfortable with its own chaos and failure | Self-deprecating in a way that sounds like shame |
| **Warm** | Inviting, community-minded, never punches down | Sarcasm at the audience's expense, exclusionary jokes |
| **Chaotic but grounded** | The absurdity is intentional; there's always a through-line | Random for random's sake, no payoff |
| **Knowledgeable but accessible** | Shares real technical information in plain language | Gatekeeping, jargon without explanation, condescension |

## Voice by Context

### Stream Game Narration (Starship Shamples)

**Register:** Dramatic narrator with a knowing wink. Slightly over-the-top. Fast-paced. The narrator is in on the joke.

**Key qualities:**
- Each stage is 1–4 sentences maximum — readable aloud in one breath
- Build tension before choices, then undercut it with absurdity
- Treat failure as dramatic and hilarious simultaneously
- Running jokes are callbacks, not footnotes — lean into them
- The crew (chat) is always the protagonist; the characters are the chaos

**Tone examples:**

> Good: "Pedro has finished his repairs. The engine is on fire. These may be related."
>
> Good: "Captain Stretch straightens his hat. He is definitely a captain. He is certainly not a shrimp. He would like you to stop asking."
>
> Avoid: "Pedro attempted to fix the engine but unfortunately caused further damage to the ship's systems."

**Running jokes (always use these consistently):**
- Pedro caused the problem (even when he didn't, he probably did)
- Duck's drinks are a terrible idea that will definitely be consumed anyway
- Captain Stretch is a shrimp and will deny it with increasing urgency
- Clone appears unexpectedly with no explanation
- The Chaos Meter climbs because of course it does

---

### Chat Bot Responses (Twitch)

**Register:** Short, punchy, character-consistent. The bot speaks as part of the world, not as a generic notification system.

**Key qualities:**
- Maximum 1–2 sentences for any automated response
- Reference the game world where it fits naturally (not forced)
- Warm and community-forward — acknowledge the person, not just the action
- Never robotic or corporate — this is a personality, not a service

**Tone examples:**

> Good: "Transmission received. Welcome aboard, {username}. Try not to listen to Duck."
>
> Good: "{username} just subscribed. The crew thanks you. Pedro is already trying to fix something in your honor."
>
> Avoid: "Thank you for subscribing! Your support means a lot to us!"

**Special cases:**
- Hype train: The ship is accelerating. This is probably fine.
- Bits: Fuel added to the reactor. Pedro is unsupervised near it.
- Raids: Incoming vessel. Crew, look alive.

### Stream Titles & Descriptions

**Register:** Specific, honest, and slightly absurd. Tells you exactly what's happening on stream today without overselling it.

**Key qualities:**
- Specific to what is actually being built/worked on — no vague titles
- A hint of the chaos to come, but not manufactured hype
- Avoid clickbait — the honest version is more interesting
- Short — a good title is 6–10 words with one punchy phrase

**Tone examples:**

> Good: "Trying a thing with the suspension that probably won't work | Starship Shamples"
>
> Good: "Installing [part] the wrong way first, then the right way | Shamples"
>
> Good: "Three-hour build became a three-week problem. Let's talk about it."
>
> Avoid: "INSANE BUILD HACK CHANGES EVERYTHING 🔥🔥🔥"
>
> Avoid: "Chill stream vibes"

---

### Community Messaging (Discord, Community Posts)

**Register:** Direct, warm, low-friction. Speaks to regulars and newcomers the same way — inclusive by default.

**Key qualities:**
- Celebrates attempts, not just successes
- Acknowledges the chaos without dwelling on it
- Invites participation explicitly — the community shapes this, they should know that
- Neurodivergent-aware: no "why didn't you just..." energy, no pressure-based urgency

**Tone examples:**

> Good: "Stream tonight. Picking back up on the build from last week. It got weird. We're going back in."
>
> Good: "The suspension thing didn't work. Documenting why for anyone who wants to try it differently."
>
> Avoid: "Don't miss the stream tonight! So much exciting content coming your way!"
>
> Avoid: "Sorry for the delay, we'll do better next time."

**Neurodivergent-aware language notes:**
- Never use shame language about missed streams or incomplete projects — the community gets it
- "We figured it out eventually" is better than "sorry it took so long"
- Celebrate the tangent, not just the goal: "went down a rabbit hole about [thing] and now we know too much about it"

---

### Social Clips & Highlights

**Register:** Hook first, depth second. The chaos catches the eye; the knowledge keeps the viewer.

**Key qualities:**
- The clip title/caption should be the funniest or most honest version of what just happened
- Don't oversell — "this is insane" works against authenticity
- Show the failure AND what comes after — context makes it real
- Clips of Pedro causing problems are always good

**Tone examples:**

> Good: "Fixed it. Made it worse. Fixed it again. We're back."
>
> Good: "This is the third time this part has come off. We're learning."
>
> Avoid: "You won't believe what happened on stream!"

## Language Dos and Don'ts

### Do
- Use specific technical terms accurately (then explain them if needed)
- Say "I don't know yet" or "we're figuring this out" — uncertainty is honest
- Reference the game characters and world in bot/chat output where natural
- Celebrate the weird approach, the unconventional fix, the experiment
- Write for someone who is new AND for someone who's been watching for months simultaneously

### Don't
- Use hype language ("INSANE," "UNBELIEVABLE," "YOU WON'T BELIEVE")
- Make the audience feel like they're missing out if they're not subscribed
- Over-apologize or perform humility — "wow so many amazing viewers" energy is not this brand
- Gatekeep: "as anyone who knows cars would know" — no, explain it
- Use shame about chaos or failure — it's the experience, not the exception
- Generic "thank you for your support!" language — make it personal to the moment

## The Metaphor in Voice

Because the brand's soul is the neurodivergent experience, the voice should occasionally make this visible — not as a disclaimer, but as a celebration.

**Making the metaphor visible in practice:**

> "The Chaos Meter is climbing. Relatable."

> "Pedro made it worse. As planned."

> "Three hours in and we're somehow further from the goal than when we started. This is the content."

> "The ship is still flying. Barely. We're counting that as a win."

These moments acknowledge the chaos without wallowing in it. They invite the community to laugh at the experience they recognize in themselves.

---

## What Good Brand Voice Produces

A cold reader who encounters this brand's voice — in a chat bot message, a stream title, a community post, or a story narration — should feel:

1. **Like they found something real** — not polished content, not brand-managed output
2. **Like they're welcome here** — regardless of budget, background, or neurology
3. **Like something interesting is happening** — the chaos is the draw, not a warning
4. **Like they want to know what happens next** — the ship is always mid-mission
